You just found out your website is down — maybe a customer texted you, maybe you tried to look something up yourself and got an error. Either way, you need to know two things fast: is it actually down, and whose problem is it to fix.

Here's how to work through it without needing an IT background.

## Step 1: Confirm it's actually down (not just your device)

Before you panic, rule out the simplest explanation. Try your site from your phone on cellular data (not your office Wi-Fi), or ask a friend to check it from their own connection. If it loads for them, the problem is local to your network or device, not your website.

If it's down for everyone, move to the next step.

## Step 2: Figure out what kind of "down" this is

Not all outages look the same, and what you see is actually a clue:

- **The page won't load at all / times out.** Usually a hosting problem — your web host's server is down, overloaded, or your site has run out of resources.
- **"This site can't be reached" / DNS error.** Your domain isn't pointing anywhere correctly. This can happen after a domain renewal issue, a DNS change, or your domain registration expiring without you noticing.
- **A security warning ("Your connection is not private").** Your SSL certificate has expired or is misconfigured. The site might still technically be "up," but browsers will block visitors from reaching it, which functionally feels the same as being down.
- **"Account suspended" or a page from your host.** Usually a billing or terms-of-service issue with your hosting provider, not a technical failure at all.

## Step 3: Check the boring stuff first

In order of how often these turn out to be the actual cause:

1. **Did a payment fail?** Hosting and domain renewals are the single most common cause of a small business site going down, and it's almost never announced loudly. Log into your hosting account and domain registrar directly (don't click email links) and check for a suspended account or a failed charge.
2. **Did your domain expire?** Domain registration lapses quietly, often with only an email warning weeks in advance that gets missed. Check your registrar account for the actual expiration date.
3. **Did your SSL certificate expire?** These typically renew automatically, but not always — and when they don't, visitors get a scary security warning instead of your site.
4. **Did you or your web designer change something recently?** A recent update, plugin change, or DNS edit is a common trigger, especially if the outage started right after.

## Step 4: If it's your host's problem, say so — don't guess

If you've ruled out payment and domain issues, contact your hosting provider's support and describe exactly what you're seeing (the specific error message matters). They can usually tell you within minutes whether it's a server-side outage on their end.

## The real fix: find out before your customers do

The frustrating part of all of this is that by the time you notice, you've often already lost the customers who tried to visit while it was down — and you have no idea how long it had been broken before you found out.

That's the actual problem worth solving: not "how do I fix an outage," but "how do I find out the moment it starts, instead of hours or days later." Automated monitoring checks your site continuously and emails you the moment something changes, along with what specifically broke and in plain language what to check next — rather than you finding out from a confused customer.
